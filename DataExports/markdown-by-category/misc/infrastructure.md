- #actionable #specific “ThunderPledge” – a website like Kickstarter for pledging to take collective actions when you reach a critical mass. For instance, there are a large number of people who would consider becoming vegetarian for climate reasons, but feel like it's pointless unless millions of others do the same. Using ThunderPledge you could pledge to become vegetarian only when a million other people have also committed the same. At that point, not only would you know for certain that your change is part of making a difference, but you would have a ready-made support group of people all trying to do the same thing.

This generalizes to many  applications – for instance organizing a boycott. Boycotts are useless unless they reach critical mass. (A friend called this “Kickstopper”!). Additionally it could perhaps be worth making a variant “ThunderVote” specifically dedicated to the voting use case. If you are, for instance, a democrat in a largely Republican area, you might think it's useless for you to get out and vote. But using this site, you could mark who you’d vote for with the touch of a button, but only pledge to actually make it to the polls if a thousand other people of your same political leanings in your district do the same.

## My Thoughts (Park)
Fundamentally, I see the big picture as one of taming information. I am inspired by the book Taming Text. I was pointed at that book when I mentioned at an Apache Solr meetup that I was building a platform I called SolrSherlock - a Watson-like agent system - on Solr; the book’s co-author told me his book was available in early access so I bought it. It was clear, concise, and very helpful. I later stopped using Solr and migrated to ElasticSearch, so I renamed the project to be less tech-centric to OpenSherlock.

The book concept generalizes from taming text to taming information, but in that space, we must consider a primary engine of information creation: humans. And, the prime mover of information by humans is conversation, so my game is really one of taming conversations.  Guild processes in the context of epic quests, for me, serve that purpose.

[Here is a book review for Total Engagement](https://pdfs.semanticscholar.org/0593/f007da6844146524b5e45c151fbc4a004f8e.pdf?_ga=2.242354308.702711303.1600376255-1444089759.1600376255). which is about this entire concept.

Thoughts about talk - Chris Barnes

The talk was genuinely incredible. I found Jack’s framework of work as a series of “quests” that are performed with a “guild” to be particularly insightful, and have been pondering it for the past few weeks. I think that framing work as a progression through a series of quests is beneficial for a series of reasons:
- It is a framework that encourages thoughtful selection, and appreciation for the work that you are currently pursuing. It’s not just a job, every single project is a quest that is undertaken with a group of dedicated guild-mates for a specific purpose.
- For me, it frames life as an epic adventure. Thinking of myself as an adventurer going on joint missions with fellow adventurers draws a personal parallel to the stories that I’ve read in the past about heroes. The Quest framework allows me personally to embody the hero position, but in an incredibly humble way, as I’m a hero surrounded by other purposeful adventuring heroes as well.
