### Noticing and releasing thoughts

[From The Mind Illuminated: A Complete Meditation Guide Integrating Buddhist Wisdom and Brain](https://www.amazon.com/Mind-Illuminated-Meditation-Integrating-Mindfulness/dp/1501156985)

One thing to add to these basic meditation instructions in Mind Illuminated. For me, what the instructions don’t answer is “why do this anyway?“
In qigong, the whole purpose of “power standing” meditation (zhanzhuang) to “build energy” in the body, until your fingers and body feel like they are brimming with life and wellness.
I find the practice of “noticing a thought and letting it go” a mental way of doing same. Noticing something I was about to go do and letting it go enables me to “re-capture” the mental and physical energy that I was about to go spend on that thought or action, and then either bask in it or repurpose it for something else. It’s all part of the endeavor of “not letting the energy escape“ or transmuting transient sparks of directed motivation into a purer abstract “energy”. This also is the point of the principle of “forbearance.” It’s not some sort of severe austerity, it’s about getting the energy you were about to spend back, and thereby “building energy.”        
A further interesting observation: it seems the more distractible you are, the faster you can progress in this practice (though it may be more challenging): you have more sparks you can “notice and release” and recapture the energy of.
